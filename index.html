About NJROS

Regulating Community Dispute Resolution Without Abolishing It

NJROS establishes a statutory, digitally enabled regulatory framework for organized community dispute forums that issue written or binding decisions — while preserving the supremacy of courts and police.

The Government of Pakistan proposes NJROS to address a critical governance gap: unregulated organized community dispute forums — commonly termed jirgas, panchayats, faislo councils, and analogous bodies — have in documented cases facilitated unlawful settlements in matters involving sexual offences, homicide, forced marriage, Swara, Vani, inheritance deprivation, and coerced land transfer.

NJROS shall not regulate informal gatherings, social meetings, religious congregations, or political assemblies. It shall not create a parallel judicial system. Courts remain supreme; police retain exclusive criminal investigation powers.

The system comprises mandatory registration, a national digital platform, five independent case workflows, an independent oversight authority (NCDOA), and comprehensive rights and integrity frameworks aligned with the Constitution of Pakistan, 1973.

Case CS-007 Latest Case Criminal / Sexual Violence

Badaber Gang Rape and Jirga Cover-Up (2026)

Badaber, Peshawar · Khyber Pakhtunkhwa · June 2026

Reported (June 2026): A 15-year-old madrassa student was allegedly gang-raped in Badaber, Peshawar. Instead of police reporting, a local jirga allegedly ordered that one of three accused marry the victim and pay compensation to her father — suppressing the matter as an "honour" issue for approximately 35 days. Police registered an FIR and arrested all three suspects and several jirga members only after a compensation payment dispute. Covered by The News International, Geo News, The Nation, ARY News, and Minute Mirror.

Source Links

  • The News International — FIR registered 35 days after incident (25 June 2026)
  • The News International — FIR report with additional complainant detail (25 June 2026)
  • The News International — All suspects arrested; jirga facilitators in custody (26 June 2026)
  • The Nation — Three arrested in Badaber case (25 June 2026)
  • ARY News — Police break up jirga settlement in rape case (25 June 2026)
  • Minute Mirror — Police crack down on illegal jirga (25 June 2026)
  • Geo News — SSP Farhan Khan statements reported via The News International and follow-up coverage (25–26 June 2026); no standalone Geo News article URL independently verified at time of compilation.

Multi-Source Reporting Synthesis

Reported: Five Pakistani news outlets published overlapping accounts between 25 and 26 June 2026. All agree on core facts: a minor madrassa student was allegedly gang-raped in Badaber; a local jirga attempted a marriage-and-compensation settlement; police intervened after a payment dispute; three suspects and jirga members were arrested. The News International and SSP Farhan Khan (via Geo News) specify a 35-day FIR delay and "honour" suppression. The Nation and ARY News describe the assault as occurring "a few months ago." Minute Mirror reports that jirga participants included the victim's father and characterises the delay as caused by "external interference." No outlet published the victim's name; no judicial findings have been reported. Allegations remain subject to investigation and trial.

Background

Reported: A 15-year-old female madrassa (seminary) student was allegedly gang-raped in the Badaber area on the outskirts of Peshawar, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (The News International, 25 June 2026). According to the FIR lodged by the victim's father at Badaber Police Station, the principal suspect allegedly intercepted the girl while she was walking to the madrassa and lured her to his nearby house (The News). One variant of The News reporting states she was lured "under the pretext of taking clothes" (The News, alternate print edition). Once inside, the principal suspect and two accomplices who were already present allegedly gang-raped the minor (The News, Nation, ARY). Senior Superintendent of Police (Operations) Farhan Khan told Geo News that the victim was under 18 years of age (The News). The victim's family reportedly did not approach police immediately after the assault (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror).

FIR and Complainant Details

Reported: FIR lodged by the victim's father at Badaber Police Station, Peshawar (The News, Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror). Registration occurred nearly 35 days after the alleged assault per The News and SSP Farhan Khan (Geo News). The Nation and ARY describe the incident as having taken place "a few months ago" — a minor discrepancy in elapsed-time reporting that may reflect rounding or additional unreported delay. At the time of initial FIR registration, SSP Farhan Khan stated suspects had been identified but were "on the run" (The News, Geo News). All three were subsequently arrested (Nation, ARY, The News follow-up, 26 June 2026).

Timeline of Events

Reported chronology (synthesised from cited sources):

  • Pre-June 2026 (approx. 35 days before FIR): Alleged gang rape of 15-year-old madrassa student in Badaber; principal suspect allegedly lured victim to his house en route to madrassa; two accomplices allegedly participated (The News FIR account).
  • Post-assault (date unreported): Matter not reported to police; instead taken before a local jirga convening accused parties and the victim's family (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror). Minute Mirror reports the victim's father participated in the jirga alongside organisers.
  • Jirga decision (date unreported): Council allegedly decreed: (1) the principal accused would marry the victim; (2) monetary compensation would be paid to her father (The News, Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror, Geo News via The News).
  • Pre-25 June 2026: Dispute reportedly arose over payment of the agreed compensation amount; following the disagreement, the victim's father approached police (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror).
  • 25 June 2026 (Wednesday): FIR registered at Badaber Police Station. SSP Farhan Khan told Geo News the 35-day delay occurred because the matter was "initially suppressed as an issue of honour" and that a jirga had attempted marriage-based resolution (The News). SSP announced a separate investigation team to probe jirga members for facilitating cover-up (The News).
  • 25 June 2026: Police arrested three suspects immediately upon FIR registration (Nation, ARY). Several jirga members detained for questioning (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror).
  • 26 June 2026 (Thursday): The News reported all three gang-rape suspects in custody; persons who facilitated the jirga between suspects and the victim's father also arrested; investigators told Geo News the case was being examined from multiple angles (The News follow-up).

Community Decision (if reported)

Reported: A local jirga — an informal council of community elders — allegedly convened after the assault to settle the matter outside the formal legal system (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror). The jirga allegedly brought together the accused individuals and the victim's family; Minute Mirror reports the victim's father was among those present. The council's reported decree had two components: forced marriage of the principal accused to the victim, and a monetary payment to her father as compensation (The News, Nation, ARY, Geo News via The News). SSP Farhan Khan characterised the jirga as having attempted to "hush up" a serious criminal matter (The News). The Nation reported authorities warned that criminal cases must be dealt with through the legal system and that serious offences must not be resolved through informal arrangements.

Investigative Findings (as reported)

Reported: During initial police investigation, authorities found evidence suggesting there had been prior contact between the suspects and the victim before the assault (Nation, ARY). Police stated investigations were continuing to determine all aspects of the case and establish the facts surrounding the allegations (Nation, ARY). Investigators told Geo News the case was being examined from multiple angles (The News, 26 June 2026). No forensic, medical, or DNA findings have been reported in cited journalism at time of compilation. Status of any medical examination of the victim has not been publicly reported.

Key Officials and Institutional Response

Reported: SSP (Operations) Farhan Khan, Peshawar Police — publicly commented on the 35-day delay, honour-based suppression, jirga cover-up, suspect identification, and formation of a separate investigation team against jirga members (The News, citing Geo News). Badaber Police Station — registered FIR and conducted initial arrests (Nation, ARY, The News). Peshawar Police — arrested three suspects and detained jirga members (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror). No reported statements from the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa provincial government, judiciary, or Human Rights Commission of Pakistan specifically on this case at time of compilation.

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

An informal council allegedly attempted to settle an alleged gang rape of a minor through forced marriage and monetary compensation — treating a non-compoundable sexual offence as a private, honour-based dispute remediable by marriage and payment. Forced marriage of a rape survivor to an accused perpetrator is prohibited under the Prevention of Anti-Women Practices (Criminal Law Amendment) Act, 2011 (Section 310A — giving a female in marriage or otherwise in badal-i-sulh). Gang rape of a minor carries enhanced penalties under the Pakistan Penal Code. The alleged jirga proceeding contravenes PLD 2019 SC 218, in which the Supreme Court held that illegal jirgas adjudicating criminal matters — including sexual offences — violate the Constitution and directed law-enforcement to act proprio motu. The approximately 35-day reporting delay, attributed by SSP Farhan Khan to honour-based suppression, obstructed timely investigation, evidence preservation, and medical examination. The case is consistent with documented patterns in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa where jirgas broker marriage settlements after sexual violence — a practice criticised by rights advocates and previously addressed in superior courts (see contextual note: separate 2022 Swat case in which Peshawar High Court accepted a jirga-brokered marriage settlement — not this case, but illustrative of regional enforcement gaps).

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Pakistan Penal Code, 1860 — Sections 375–376 (rape); Section 376(2) (gang rape); Section 109 (abetment); Section 366 (kidnapping/abduction from lawful guardianship, if applicable); Sections 354/354A (assault/criminal force to woman). Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 — FIR registration and investigation duties. Prevention of Anti-Women Practices (Criminal Law Amendment) Act, 2011 — Section 310A (prohibition on giving females in marriage as badal-i-sulh); Section 498C (prohibition of depriving woman of inheritance, if applicable). Protection of Women (Criminal Laws Amendment) Act, 2006. Criminal Law (Amendment) (Offence of Rape) Act, 2021 — special procedures for rape trials. Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929. Juvenile Justice System Act, 2018 (victim as minor). Judicial precedent: PLD 2019 SC 218 (Suo Motu Case No. 6 of 2014) — illegal jirgas, criminal matter adjudication prohibition, proprio motu FIR direction, joint liability for aiders.

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Article 9 (security of person); Article 14 (dignity of person — honour-based suppression); Article 19-A (right to information, if state institutions failed to act); Article 25 (equality before law and non-discrimination); Article 25A (rights of the child); Article 10A (fair trial and access to courts); Article 35 (protection of marriage and family — invoked against forced marriage); Article 37(e) (state duty to protect women and children); Article 175 (judiciary and court paramountcy over informal forums).

Government Response

Reported: Peshawar Police registered FIR, arrested three suspects, and detained jirga members (Nation, ARY, The News). SSP Farhan Khan publicly confirmed honour-based suppression, announced charges against jirga members, and constituted a separate investigation team for the cover-up (The News, Geo News). Authorities reiterated that criminal cases must be dealt with through the legal system and warned against resolving serious offences through informal arrangements (Nation). Minute Mirror reported police emphasised that parallel justice mechanisms are unlawful and that all organisers of the forced-marriage pact face formal investigation. No provincial legislation, cabinet statement, or parliamentary action specific to this incident has been reported in cited sources.

Police Action

Reported: FIR registered at Badaber Police Station following the father's complaint after the compensation dispute (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror). Three suspects arrested immediately upon registration (Nation, ARY, 25 June 2026). All three confirmed in custody by 26 June 2026 (The News follow-up). Several jirga members who allegedly participated in deciding the case outside the legal framework detained for questioning (Nation, ARY, The News). Persons who facilitated the jirga between suspects and the victim's father taken into custody (The News, 26 June 2026). Separate investigation team formed against jirga members for facilitating cover-up (The News, SSP Farhan Khan). Broader investigation ongoing at time of reporting (Nation, ARY, Minute Mirror).

Court Proceedings

Reported: No trial, conviction, acquittal, or bail proceedings have been reported in cited sources as of 26 June 2026. Criminal investigation in progress. Police noted prior contact between suspects and victim (Nation, ARY) — factual and legal characterisation subject to judicial determination. If prosecuted, the case would likely fall within the jurisdiction of sessions courts and potentially special gender-based violence courts notified under the Anti-Rape Act, 2021 — but court allocation has not been reported.

Current Status

Reported (as of 26 June 2026): All three alleged perpetrators arrested (The News). Jirga facilitators in custody (The News, Nation, ARY). Investigation and prosecution continuing. No court outcome reported. Case remains at investigation stage. Illustrates continued post-PLD 2019 SC 218 enforcement gaps in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, where illegal jirgas allegedly continue to adjudicate sexual offences against minors and suppress police reporting through honour-based community pressure.

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

The alleged assault would trigger mandatory criminal-incident workflow with priority police notification within two hours — before any jirga could lawfully convene on the matter. NJROS would prohibit registration or recording of marriage-based "settlements" for sexual offences; any attempt to log such a decree would generate an automatic NCDOA and law-enforcement alert. Unregistered jirga proceedings on criminal matters would be unlawful per PLD 2019 SC 218; NCDOA would receive digital incident flags, inspect forum participants, and refer jirga members — including any family members who participated — for prosecution under joint-liability principles. Digital timestamped records would document the ~35-day suppression gap identified by SSP Farhan Khan, enabling post-incident accountability review of why no proprio motu police action occurred during the jirga settlement period. Mandatory minor-victim safeguarding protocols would require immediate child-protection referral independent of family or jirga preferences.

Lessons for Policy Reform

This June 2026 case is among the most comprehensively reported contemporary examples of illegal jirga intervention in a sexual violence matter in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. Police action occurred only after a compensation payment dispute — not at the point of assault, not when the jirga convened, and not when a forced-marriage decree was issued. The 35-day honour-based suppression window documented by SSP Farhan Khan represents a systemic failure of the proprio motu reporting duty articulated in PLD 2019 SC 218. Multi-source reporting (five outlets in 48 hours) suggests significant public interest, yet no judicial or provincial policy response had been reported at time of compilation. NJROS mandatory forum registration, real-time criminal referral, prohibition of marriage settlements for sexual offences, and NCDOA oversight are designed to collapse the suppression window and create auditable records before informal forums can purport to "resolve" non-compoundable crimes against children.

Case CS-001 Criminal / Sexual Violence

Meerwala Village Council Order (2002)

Meerwala, Muzaffargarh District · Punjab · June 2002

A village council (reported as a panchayat/jirga) allegedly ordered a punitive sexual assault as retaliation for a dispute involving a minor male relative. The case became internationally documented after the complainant pursued formal legal proceedings.

Source Links

  • BBC News — Mukhtar Mai: History of a Rape Case (timeline)
  • BBC News — Supreme Court acquittals (21 April 2011)
  • Reuters — Story of rape serves timely reminder to Pakistan
  • Al Jazeera — Supreme Court upholds acquittals (21 April 2011)

Background

Reported: In June 2002, a dispute arose in Meerwala village, southern Punjab, between the complainant's family and the influential Mastoi clan. The Mastoi clan allegedly accused the complainant's 12-year-old brother of an improper association with a Mastoi woman. The complainant's family reportedly stated the accusation was false and that the boy had been sexually assaulted by Mastoi men (BBC, 2005 timeline).

Timeline of Events

Reported: 22 June 2002 — A village council convened; a proposed settlement involving marriages between families was reportedly rejected by the Mastoi side, who allegedly insisted on a retaliatory principle (BBC). The complainant was summoned, allegedly apologised, and was thereafter gang-raped by four men in a nearby structure (BBC). 28 June 2002 — The local imam reportedly denounced the act and encouraged reporting to police (BBC). 31 August 2002 — Trial court convicted six men: four for rape and two for participation in the council that decreed the assault (BBC). March 2005 — Lahore High Court acquitted five and commuted one sentence to life imprisonment (Al Jazeera). 21 April 2011 — Supreme Court upheld acquittal of five; one man remained under life sentence (BBC, Al Jazeera).

Community Decision (if reported)

Reported: A village council allegedly decreed that the complainant be subjected to gang rape as punishment related to her brother's alleged conduct. BBC reported that two men were convicted specifically for participation in the panchayat that decreed the rape.

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

A non-state council allegedly ordered a sexual assault — a non-compoundable criminal offence — as collective punishment. The case illustrated how informal forums can purport to adjudicate criminal matters and impose penalties reserved to courts and the criminal justice system.

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Pakistan Penal Code, 1860 (offences of rape and abetment); Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898; Anti-Terrorism Act, 1997 (trial initially conducted in anti-terrorism court per Reuters reporting).

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Article 9 (security of person); Article 14 (dignity); Article 25 (equality); Article 10A (fair trial and due process); Article 175 (judiciary and court jurisdiction).

Government Response

Reported: Punjab government directed police investigation after media coverage; prosecution proceeded in anti-terrorism court (Reuters, BBC).

Police Action

Reported: Police investigated after imam and media intervention; chargesheet filed against 14 suspects in July 2002 (Al Jazeera).

Court Proceedings

Judicial findings: Trial court (August 2002) convicted six. Judicial findings: Lahore High Court (2005) acquitted five. Judicial findings: Supreme Court (21 April 2011) upheld acquittals citing lack of evidence including absence of DNA testing (BBC, NPR reporting).

Current Status

Judicial finding: Final Supreme Court judgment of 2011; five accused acquitted, one serving life sentence per court record. Case closed in superior courts. HRCP and international human rights organisations criticised the outcome (Al Jazeera).

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

Any forum convening to address a criminal allegation would be required to register on the NJROS platform. The criminal-incident workflow would trigger immediate police notification (priority protocol within two hours). The platform would prohibit recording of any "settlement" of a non-compoundable sexual offence. NCDOA would receive an auditable incident record; unregistered forums issuing binding decisions would face inspection and referral powers.

Lessons for Policy Reform

Documented absence of real-time oversight allowed an alleged council decree to be executed without statutory intervention. Subsequent prosecution demonstrated that courts can convict council participants — but delayed, inconsistent appellate outcomes underscore the need for mandatory digital records, immediate criminal referral, and independent oversight at the point of forum convening rather than after media exposure.

Case CS-002 Criminal / Sexual Violence

Multan Revenge Rape Order (2017)

Multan area, Punjab · July 2017

Reported: An informal village council allegedly ordered that a 16-year-old girl be raped in retaliation for an alleged sexual assault committed by her brother against a 12-year-old girl. Police arrested 25 persons; the Supreme Court took notice.

Source Links

  • Reuters — Pakistani council orders 'revenge rape' (27 July 2017)
  • BBC — Pakistan village council orders 'revenge rape' (27 July 2017)
  • Al Jazeera — More arrests; HRCP comment (27 July 2017)

Background

Reported: A family alleged that a 16-year-old boy raped a 12-year-old neighbour. Families reportedly convened an informal council. Reuters and BBC reported that medical examinations confirmed rape in both cases (BBC, 27 July 2017).

Timeline of Events

Reported: Council convened in Multan area (Reuters). 17 July 2017 — Council allegedly ordered that the sister of the accused boy be handed to the first victim's brother to be raped; the act was reportedly carried out after her family handed her over (Reuters, BBC). Mothers of both girls subsequently filed police complaints (BBC). Police arrested 25 council participants (Reuters). Chief Justice of Pakistan ordered high-level investigation (Al Jazeera).

Community Decision (if reported)

Reported: Council ruled that the sister of the alleged first perpetrator be raped as retribution. BBC noted that some sources characterised the gathering as family members rather than a traditional community jirga, while police attributed the order to a village council.

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

An informal body allegedly ordered and facilitated a second sexual assault as collective punishment, treating a woman as fungible compensation for a male relative's alleged crime — contrary to statutory criminal law and fundamental rights.

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Pakistan Penal Code, 1860 (rape, abetment); Prevention of Anti-Women Practices (Criminal Law Amendment) Act, 2011; Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898.

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Articles 9, 14, 25, 10A, and 35 (protection of marriage and family within law).

Government Response

Reported: Supreme Court requested a report; Chief Justice ordered investigation (Reuters, Al Jazeera). Punjab police conducted arrests.

Police Action

Reported: Cases registered against council members under provisions relating to forcing a woman into marriage and abetment (Al Jazeera). 25 arrests reported (Reuters); further arrests reported (Al Jazeera).

Court Proceedings

Reported: Criminal cases registered; long-term judicial outcomes not widely reported in the cited sources at time of publication.

Current Status

Reported: Prosecution initiated in 2017. HRCP chairperson Mehdi Hasan noted at the time that culprits in similar prior cases had rarely been punished (Al Jazeera).

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

Registration of any convened forum would be mandatory. Both criminal allegations would enter the criminal-incident workflow with simultaneous police notification. The platform would block any recorded "reconciliation" involving sexual offences. NCDOA would flag the case for priority inspection; digital records would preserve chain-of-custody for prosecution.

Lessons for Policy Reform

Fifteen years after Meerwala, a structurally similar council-ordered assault was still reported (Reuters). This indicates that landmark judgments alone do not prevent recurrence without registration, mandatory referral, and enforceable oversight at the point of forum assembly.

Case CS-003 Criminal / Honour Killing

Kohistan Video Honour Killings (2011–2019)

Kohistan District · Khyber Pakhtunkhwa · 2011–2019

Alleged then partially confirmed: Women filmed at a wedding were allegedly ordered killed by a tribal jirga. A multi-year investigation involving the Supreme Court eventually led to life sentences for three men in 2019, while earlier proceedings were complicated by disputed evidence and further killings.

Source Links

  • BBC — Three guilty in 'honour killing' blood feud (September 2019)
  • Dawn — Afzal's killing editorial (6 March 2019)
  • Dawn — SC holds jirgas violative (16 January 2019)
  • Amnesty International — Tribal council impunity statement (2023)

Background

Reported: A video emerged in 2011 appearing to show women singing and clapping at a wedding alongside men in Kohistan, a conservative district where such conduct was reported as culturally sensitive (BBC, 2019). Afzal Kohistani, brother of men appearing in the video, publicly alleged that a tribal jirga ordered the women killed (BBC).

Timeline of Events

Reported: 2011 — Video surfaces. 2012 — Allegations reach national media; Supreme Court orders investigation; officials initially produced women claimed to be those in the video (BBC). 2014–2016 — Further petitions; inconsistencies in identity of women produced noted (Dawn). August 2018 — FIR registered on Supreme Court order (BBC). December 2018 — Investigation reportedly found three women had been killed (BBC). 2019 — Three men received life sentences; five acquitted (BBC). Afzal Kohistani, who campaigned for justice, was murdered in 2019 after years of threats (Dawn).

Community Decision (if reported)

Alleged: Azadkhel tribe jirga allegedly ordered participants in the video to be killed to restore family honour (BBC, Dawn). Reported: Dawn editorial cited allegations that a jirga later ordered Afzal Kohistani's killing.

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

An informal council allegedly imposed death sentences for perceived moral offences — a power reserved to courts under Article 175. The case also demonstrated obstruction of investigation, alleged presentation of substitute persons, and retaliation against a whistleblower (Dawn).

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Pakistan Penal Code, 1860 (murder, honour-related violence); Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898; Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, 2011.

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Article 9 (life and liberty); Article 14 (dignity); Article 10A (fair trial); Article 19 (whistleblower retaliation); Article 175.

Government Response

Reported: Supreme Court suo motu and commission investigations over multiple years (BBC, Dawn). Police registered FIR in 2018 per court order.

Police Action

Reported: Delayed FIR registration; investigation eventually confirmed three deaths (BBC). Local officials initially denied killings (BBC).

Court Proceedings

Judicial finding: Besham court (2019) sentenced three men to life imprisonment; five acquitted (BBC). Amnesty International later reported some accused were acquitted in related proceedings citing lack of evidence (2023 statement — status may have evolved).

Current Status

Reported: Partial convictions in 2019; whistleblower Afzal Kohistani killed March 2019 (Dawn). Amnesty (2023) cited continuing impunity concerns in Kohistan for similar jirga-ordered killings.

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

Any jirga convening after a criminal or honour-related incident would require registration and risk-flagging. Whistleblower reports would enter a protected NCDOA complaint channel. Criminal allegations would trigger mandatory police notification; the platform would maintain tamper-evident records to reduce scope for substitute-person cover-ups during investigations.

Lessons for Policy Reform

Even with Supreme Court involvement, justice took eight years and was incomplete. Absence of contemporaneous digital records enabled disputed identities and delayed FIR registration. NJROS-style mandatory record-keeping and criminal referral at first convening could shorten the evidentiary gap that obstructed prosecution.

Case CS-004 Family / Swara (Child Marriage)

Upper Dir Child Swara Order (2015)

Hattan Dara, Upper Dir · Khyber Pakhtunkhwa · March 2015

Reported: A jirga allegedly ordered a four-year-old girl be married to a two-and-a-half-year-old boy to settle a family dispute. Police arrested eight jirga members but were reportedly unable to sustain charges under Section 310-A PPC due to non-cognizable status of the offence.

Source Links

  • Dawn — Existing laws support suspects in swara cases (31 March 2015)
  • Dawn — Superior courts focus on swara victims

Background

Reported: A dispute between two families in Hattan Dara, Dir police station jurisdiction, was reportedly referred to a jirga (Dawn, 31 March 2015).

Timeline of Events

Reported: Jirga decided to marry a four-year-old girl to a two-and-a-half-year-old boy to settle the dispute. 26 March 2015 — Uncle Bacha Gul lodged a police complaint alleging the rival group was forcing the marriage. Police arrested eight jirga members under Section 310-A PPC. District prosecutor advised police that the 2011 amendment made the offence non-cognizable; arrests were reframed under Section 107 CrPC (breach of peace). Suspects were subsequently bailed (Dawn).

Community Decision (if reported)

Reported: Jirga ordered child marriage (swara/badal-i-sulh) between infants to resolve an inter-family dispute (Dawn).

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

Compelled child marriage as dispute currency violates Section 310-A PPC and the Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, 2011. Dawn reported that legislative design (non-cognizable offence) impeded effective police action.

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Section 310-A, Pakistan Penal Code; Prevention of Anti-Women Practices (Criminal Law Amendment) Act, 2011; Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929.

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Article 25 (non-discrimination); Article 35; Article 25A (education — child victims); Article 14 (dignity).

Government Response

Reported: Police registered case and made arrests; prosecution hampered by legal classification (Dawn). Peshawar High Court had previously directed enactment of special law for swara — reported as unimplemented (Dawn).

Police Action

Reported: Eight arrests on 26 March 2015; subsequently bailed under Section 107 CrPC rather than 310-A PPC (Dawn).

Court Proceedings

Reported: Case referred to court under CrPC complaint procedure; detailed trial outcome not reported in cited Dawn article.

Current Status

Reported: Suspects bailed at time of Dawn reporting (2015). Case cited in ongoing policy debate on making swara offences cognizable.

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

Family-mediation workflow would flag any proposed marriage involving minors as a Schedule A prohibited matter. Automatic NCDOA alert and police referral. Digital record of jirga participants would support prosecution under Section 310-A regardless of cognizability debates. Unregistered forum would face immediate compliance order.

Lessons for Policy Reform

Legislation criminalising swara exists but enforcement gaps — including non-cognizable classification reported by Dawn — allow jirga orders to proceed with limited consequence. NJROS adds operational enforcement layer: registration, digital evidence, and mandatory oversight referral independent of arrest-power technicalities.

Case CS-005 Family / Swara

Shangla Swara Order (2021)

Takhta Dandai, Bisham Tehsil, Shangla · Khyber Pakhtunkhwa · September 2021

Reported: A secret jirga ordered a 12-year-old girl given in swara and imposed a fine; police arrested seven jirga members including a prayer leader after the complainant filed a report.

Source Links

  • Dawn — Seven jirga members arrested (27 September 2021)
  • Dawn — Federal Shariat Court declares swara un-Islamic (October 2021)

Background

Reported: Local people discovered illicit relations between a 19-year-old male and a girl; a secret jirga was held on a Friday (Dawn, 27 September 2021).

Timeline of Events

Reported: Jirga engaged the girl to the 19-year-old; gave his younger sister in swara to a teenage boy of the other family; imposed Rs100,000 fine. A prayer leader solemnised the nikah of the 12-year-old swara victim. Police registered cases against nine persons on Saturday; arrested seven including Maulana Umar Habib (Dawn). Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

Compelled child marriage as dispute settlement; involvement of religious officiant in an unlawful arrangement. Practice declared un-Islamic by Federal Shariat Court weeks later (Dawn, October 2021).

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Section 310-A PPC; Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, 2011; Muslim Family Laws Ordinance, 1961 (age of marriage).

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Articles 14, 25, 35, 25A; State obligations under CEDAW and CRC.

Government Response

Reported: Shangla police arrests and investigation; district-level jirga restrictions imposed (Dawn). Federal Shariat Court declared swara un-Islamic (October 2021).

Police Action

Reported: Seven arrested; two at large at time of reporting (Dawn).

Court Proceedings

Reported: Investigation continuing at time of Dawn report; trial outcomes not cited.

Current Status

Reported: Prosecution initiated September 2021. Demonstrates swara practice continuing despite prior Supreme Court and legislative prohibitions.

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

Secret unregistered jirgas would be unlawful under NJROS. Family workflow would block swara/vani categorisation; automatic NCDOA and police notification. Registered forums would require female-safeguard compliance and written record accessible to inspection.

Lessons for Policy Reform

A complainant's courage enabled prosecution — but only after the swara was executed. Mandatory registration and pre-decision oversight could intercept prohibited orders before nikah solemnisation. District police restrictions on jirga venues are reactive; NJROS provides proactive national architecture.

Case CS-006 Institutional / Judicial Finding

NCSW v. Government of Pakistan — Supreme Court Judgment (2019)

Islamabad · Federal · 16 January 2019

Judicial finding: The Supreme Court declared jirgas and panchayats illegal to the extent they adjudicate civil or criminal matters, violate constitutional rights, and breach Pakistan's international obligations. The judgment provides systemic evidence for regulatory reform.

Source Links

  • Dawn — SC holds jirgas violative (16 January 2019)
  • The News International — Functioning of jirgas violative of Constitution
  • Judgment: PLD 2019 SC 218 (National Commission on Status of Women v. Government of Pakistan)

Background

Judicial record: Constitution petition under Article 184(3) filed by NCSW and human rights petitioners challenging jirgas/panchayats operating as adjudicating bodies awarding judgments, punishments, and decisions in family, civil, and criminal disputes (Dawn; PLD 2019 SC 218).

Timeline of Events

Judicial record: Petition heard; provincial governments submitted compliance positions. 16 January 2019 — Supreme Court judgment delivered. Subsequent hearings (reported 2020) sought provincial progress reports on illegal jirga practices (The News).

Community Decision (if reported)

Not applicable — this is a systemic constitutional challenge rather than a single forum decision. The judgment references documented patterns including swara, vani, honour killings, and inheritance deprivation through male-only deliberation (PLD 2019 SC 218; Dawn).

Why the Decision Raised Legal or Human Rights Concerns

Judicial finding: Parallel adjudication violates Articles 4, 8, 10A, 25, and 175(3); breaches UDHR, ICCPR, and CEDAW obligations. Decisions based on personal knowledge and hearsay threaten natural justice (Dawn).

Relevant Pakistani Laws

Constitution of Pakistan, 1973; Pakistan Penal Code; Code of Criminal Procedure; Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, 2011; provincial ADR legislation (distinguished from illegal jirga adjudication).

Constitutional Rights Potentially Affected

Judicial finding: Articles 4, 8, 9, 10, 10A, 14, 19, 19A, 25, 25A, 35, 37, 175 — as enumerated in the judgment (Dawn, PLD 2019 SC 218).

Government Response

Judicial direction: Law-enforcement agencies directed to file FIRs proprio motu for unreported crimes arising from illegal jirga decisions; joint liability for aiders of illegal forums (Dawn). Reported: Provincial progress reports subsequently sought (The News, 2020).

Police Action

Judicial direction: Police duty-bound to act against crimes flowing from illegal jirga directives (Dawn). Reported: HRCP and civil society documented limited enforcement on the ground post-judgment.

Court Proceedings

Judicial finding: PLD 2019 SC 218 — jirgas may act only as voluntary arbitration/mediation in civil disputes with consent; may not adjudicate criminal matters or issue binding punishments.

Current Status

Judicial finding: Binding precedent. Reported: Illegal jirga practices continued in multiple provinces after 2019 (cases CS-001 through CS-005 post-date or pre-date but illustrate enforcement gap). Federal Shariat Court reaffirmed swara prohibition (2021).

How NJROS Would Handle This Case

NJROS operationalises the 2019 judgment: distinguishes lawful mediation from illegal adjudication; mandates registration; requires digital records; establishes NCDOA inspection and referral powers; creates enforceable federal-provincial implementation architecture the judgment identified as necessary but left to executive implementation.

Lessons for Policy Reform

Judicial declarations without administrative infrastructure produce limited compliance. NJROS translates constitutional and international obligations into registrable, auditable, oversight-enforced practice — the implementation gap the Supreme Court judgment implicitly requires.

Cross-Case Analysis

Statistical Summary

  • Reported (HRCP, cited by Amnesty 2023): 384 instances of honour killings reported in Pakistan in 2022.
  • Reported (UNFPA, cited by Amnesty 2023): Conviction rate in gender-based violence cases estimated at 1–2.5%.
  • Judicial record: Supreme Court identified jirgas operating across KP, Punjab, Sindh, and Balochistan (PLD 2019 SC 218).
  • Reported (Dawn 2015): Legislative gap — Section 310-A PPC made non-cognizable under 2011 amendment, impeding police arrest without court warrant.
  • Six case records in this section span 2002–2021, demonstrating persistence across decades and provinces. Case CS-007 (June 2026) confirms the pattern continues in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.

Common Patterns

  • Informal councils convene for criminal or honour-related disputes without statutory authority.
  • Women and girls disproportionately bear punitive outcomes (swara, revenge rape, honour killing orders).
  • Secret or unrecorded proceedings delay or obstruct police and judicial intervention.
  • Prosecution often depends on individual complainant courage or media exposure rather than systematic oversight.
  • Appellate reversals and acquittals reported even where trial courts convict (CS-001).
  • Repeat incidents reported despite existing legislation (CS-002 occurring 15 years after CS-001).

Governance Failures

  • No national register of forums issuing binding decisions at time of cases.
  • No mandatory standardized digital record-keeping for forum proceedings.
  • No statutory duty to refer criminal allegations to police at point of convening.
  • No independent inspectorate with suspension powers (identified in NJROS White Paper).
  • Enforcement gap between superior court judgments and district-level practice (CS-006 vs CS-005).
  • Legislative design weaknesses (non-cognizable swara offence) reported to aid suspects (CS-004).

Human Rights Issues

  • Violations of bodily integrity and security of person (Articles 9, 14).
  • Gender discrimination in council composition and outcomes (Article 25; CEDAW).
  • Denial of fair trial and court access (Article 10A; ICCPR).
  • Child rights violations in swara cases (CRC; Article 25A).
  • Retaliation against whistleblowers and complainants (CS-003; Dawn 2019).
  • Parallel adjudication usurping court jurisdiction (Article 175).

Policy Recommendations

  • Implement NJROS registration framework for all organized forums issuing written or binding decisions, as proposed in the Cabinet White Paper (2026).
  • Mandatory criminal-incident protocol with police notification within two hours for priority cases — addressing the referral gap documented in CS-001, CS-002, and CS-003.
  • Establish NCDOA with inspection, suspension, and referral powers to close the enforcement gap identified in PLD 2019 SC 218.
  • Digital tamper-evident records to prevent evidentiary obstruction reported in CS-003 (substitute-person presentations).
  • Harmonise Section 310-A PPC cognizability — Dawn (2015) reported legislative loopholes aiding swara perpetrators; Federal Shariat Court (2021) declared swara un-Islamic but implementation gaps persist.
  • Whistleblower protection channel integrated with NCDOA — addressing retaliation documented in CS-003.
  • Pilot districts (Swabi, Kohat, Loralai, Kech, Rajanpur, Kashmore) with baseline surveys before impact claims, per Cabinet White Paper evidence standards.
  • Provincial implementation agreements for police liaison under cooperative federalism (Eighteenth Amendment framework cited in NJROS policy).
Our Solution

Why NJROS?

NJROS offers regulation without abolition — transparency, accountability, and rights protection within the constitutional framework of Pakistan.

Core Capabilities

Built for Accountability and Citizen Protection

Transparency

Mandatory digital record-keeping for all registered forum proceedings and written outcomes. Anonymized public statistics enable evidence-based policy without compromising individual privacy.

Accountability

Independent oversight through NCDOA with registration, inspection, investigation, referral, and appeals powers. Annual reporting to Parliament and biennial Human Rights Impact Assessments.

Digital Records

Tamper-evident digital case files with audit trails, NADRA identity verification, and integration with Police Record and Statistics Management System (PSRMS) for incident cross-referencing.

Police Notifications

Standardized criminal incident protocol ensures allegations are referred to police within two hours for priority cases. No automatic FIR registration — police retain statutory investigation discretion.

Human Rights Protection

Safeguards for women, children, minorities, persons with disabilities, victims, and witnesses. Prohibition on private settlement of non-compoundable offences including Swara, Vani, and forced marriage.

Constitutional Compliance

Grounded in Articles 4, 8, 9, 10, 10A, 14, 25, 37, and 175 of the Constitution. Courts remain supreme; NJROS operates as a regulatory and record-keeping layer, not a parallel judiciary.
