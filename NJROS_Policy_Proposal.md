# 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## 1.1 Purpose of This Proposal

The Government of Pakistan recognizes that **jirgas**—traditional councils for dispute resolution—remain a deeply embedded institution across multiple provinces and territories, particularly in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Balochistan, parts of Punjab and Sindh, and formerly in the erstwhile Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA), now merged districts of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. For centuries, jirgas have served communities as mechanisms of restorative justice, social reconciliation, and localized governance.

However, the absence of a standardized national framework for registration, oversight, and accountability has enabled a parallel and often opaque system of decision-making. In numerous documented instances, unregistered jirgas have facilitated outcomes that contravene the Constitution of Pakistan, statutory law, and international human rights obligations. These outcomes include the concealment of rape and murder through private settlements, the imposition of **Swara** and **Vani** (compulsory marriage of women and girls as dispute resolution), denial of inheritance rights, unlawful land dispossession, and the suppression of criminal prosecution through coerced "forgiveness."

This policy proposal recommends the establishment of the **National Jirga Registration and Oversight System (NJROS)**—a mandatory, technology-enabled regulatory framework that preserves the cultural legitimacy of legitimate dispute resolution while ensuring transparency, public safety, legal compliance, and human rights protection.

**NJROS does not seek to abolish jirgas.** Rather, it seeks to bring them within a lawful, auditable, and accountable national architecture—analogous to how the State regulates medical practitioners, lawyers, and financial institutions without eliminating those professions.

## 1.2 The Core Problem

Pakistan presently lacks:

1. A **central register** of active jirgas, their jurisdictions, membership, and case history;
2. A **mandatory reporting mechanism** for jirga proceedings and decisions;
3. An **automated linkage** between jirga activity and law enforcement where cognizable offences are implicated;
4. A **monitoring authority** empowered to suspend, investigate, and refer violations for prosecution;
5. A **public record** accessible to victims, courts, and oversight bodies.

The result is a governance vacuum in which harmful practices persist without documentary trace, victims are silenced through community pressure, and perpetrators of serious crimes evade the formal justice system.

## 1.3 The Proposed Solution

NJROS comprises five integrated pillars:

| Pillar | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mandatory Registration** | No jirga may convene or issue binding decisions without prior registration and case filing on the NJROS portal |
| **Digital Case Management** | End-to-end recording of parties, allegations, proceedings, and outcomes |
| **Criminal Case Detection** | Rule-based and AI-assisted flagging of matters involving cognizable offences |
| **Police Notification** | Automatic and mandatory alerts to district police and prosecution where criminal conduct is detected |
| **Oversight and Scoring** | A National Monitoring Authority with powers of audit, suspension, and referral; performance scoring of jirga panels |

## 1.4 Expected Outcomes

Upon full implementation, NJROS is expected to:

- Reduce unlawful settlements in cases of sexual violence, homicide, and forced marriage;
- Create an immutable digital audit trail for every registered proceeding;
- Strengthen the formal justice system by closing the "jirga loophole";
- Enhance public trust through transparent, rights-compliant dispute resolution;
- Provide policymakers with national data on community justice trends;
- Align Pakistan's customary justice practices with Articles 4, 9, 10, 10A, and 25 of the Constitution and ratified international conventions.

## 1.5 Investment and Timeline

The proposed implementation period is **36 months**, phased across federal establishment, provincial rollout, pilot districts, and national scale-up. Indicative total cost over the first five years is **PKR 4.2–5.8 billion**, including technology infrastructure, training, monitoring authority operations, and public awareness.

## 1.6 Recommendation

It is recommended that the Federal Cabinet approve NJROS in principle, designate the Ministry of Law and Justice as the lead implementing agency, establish an Inter-Provincial Steering Committee, and initiate legislative drafting for the **National Jirga Registration and Oversight Act** within ninety (90) days of approval.

# 2. BACKGROUND

## 2.1 Historical and Cultural Context

The jirga (from Pashto/Persian roots connoting assembly or council) is among the oldest institutions of dispute resolution in the regions comprising modern Pakistan. Among Pashtun communities, the jirga is governed by **Pashtunwali**—a code emphasizing hospitality (*melmastia*), sanctuary (*nanawatai*), revenge (*badal*), and collective responsibility (*nang*). Among Baloch communities, the **Jirga-e-Baloch** performs similar functions under tribal custom. In Sindh and southern Punjab, parallel institutions such as **panchayats** and **faislo** gatherings operate with comparable social authority.

Historically, jirgas have resolved land boundaries, water rights, blood feuds, commercial disputes, and family disagreements with speed and community acceptance that formal courts—often distant, costly, and procedurally complex—have struggled to match. Elders (*masharan*), religious figures, and respected notables have served as neutral arbiters, and compliance has been enforced through social sanction rather than state coercion.

## 2.2 Jirgas and the Formal State

Following independence in 1947, Pakistan inherited a dual legal order: statutory law derived from British colonial codes and living customary practice. The Constitution of Pakistan (1973) establishes the supremacy of law (Article 4) and provides for equality before law and equal protection of law (Article 25). The superior courts have repeatedly held that jirga decisions cannot override statutory rights or substitute for criminal prosecution.

Notable judicial pronouncements include:

- **Suo motu actions** by the Supreme Court of Pakistan concerning jirga-ordered honour killings and Swara;
- High Court interventions in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and Balochistan quashing jirga decisions that contravened the Prevention of Anti-Women Practices (Criminal Law Amendment) Act, 2011;
- Consistent jurisprudence that private compromise cannot extinguish the State's duty to prosecute **cognizable offences**.

Despite this jurisprudence, enforcement on the ground remains weak. Police officers, local administrators, and community members frequently treat jirga outcomes as final, particularly in rural and peripheral districts where state presence is thin and tribal or customary authority remains strong.

## 2.3 Legislative and Policy Landscape

Several laws touch upon jirga-related harms but none establish comprehensive registration or oversight:

| Instrument | Relevance |
|------------|-----------|
| Constitution of Pakistan, 1973 | Fundamental rights; access to justice |
| Pakistan Penal Code, 1860 (PPC) | Offences including murder, rape, kidnapping, wrongful confinement |
| Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 (CrPC) | Cognizable vs. non-cognizable offences; FIR procedure |
| Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, 2011 | Prohibits Swara, Vani, marriage to Quran, deprivation of inheritance |
| Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Elimination of Custom of Ghag Act, 2013 | Prohibits forced marriage claims |
| Protection of Women (Criminal Laws Amendment) Act, 2006 | Procedural reforms for women in criminal justice |
| Legal Aid and Justice Authority Act, 2020 | Potential interface for victim support |
| Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Alternate Dispute Resolution Act, 2020 | Regulated ADR—not jirga-specific |

Provincial alternate dispute resolution (ADR) frameworks regulate court-annexed or statutory mediation but **do not** address traditional jirgas operating outside judicial supervision.

## 2.4 International Obligations

Pakistan is a state party to, inter alia:

- The **International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)**;
- The **Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)**;
- The **Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)**;
- The **Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment (CAT)**.

UN treaty bodies and the Human Rights Council have repeatedly expressed concern regarding jirga-adjacent practices, particularly violence against women and girls and impunity for honour-related crimes. NJROS would materially advance Pakistan's compliance reporting under the Universal Periodic Review (UPR) and treaty body examinations.

## 2.5 Digital Governance Context

The Government of Pakistan has pursued digital transformation through the **Digital Pakistan Vision**, the **National Database and Registration Authority (NADRA)** ecosystem, the **Pakistan Citizen Portal**, and provincial e-governance initiatives. NJROS aligns with this trajectory by applying proven digital identity, case management, and inter-agency notification architectures to an underserved domain of informal justice.

## 2.6 Why Regulation, Not Prohibition

Attempts to abolish jirgas by decree have proven socially divisive and practically unenforceable. Communities perceive jirgas as legitimate expressions of self-governance. A prohibition-only approach drives jirgas further underground, worsening opacity and harm.

International best practice in plural legal systems—from Ethiopia's Shari'a courts registration to Indonesia's customary institution mapping—demonstrates that **registration plus standards** achieves greater compliance than suppression. NJROS adopts this evidence-based approach.

## 2.7 Comparative International Practice

A review of comparable jurisdictions informs NJROS design:

**Afghanistan:** Traditional *shuras* and jirgas operate with minimal state oversight, contributing to documented cases of unlawful exchange marriage and impunity for violence against women. Post-2001 reforms attempted limited integration with formal courts but lacked national registration infrastructure. NJROS learns from this gap.

**India:** *Khap panchayats* in Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, and Rajasthan have issued unlawful orders including honour-related sanctions. The Supreme Court of India has declared khap interference in marriages illegal (*Shakti Vahini v. Union of India*, 2018). India lacks a unified registration system—enforcement remains litigation-dependent. NJROS proposes preventive architecture rather than reactive judicial intervention alone.

**Kenya:** The **Traditional Dispute Resolution Act, 2012** requires registration of customary dispute resolution mechanisms and prohibits outcomes inconsistent with the Constitution. NJROS mirrors Kenya's registration mandate while adding real-time criminal detection absent in Kenyan implementation.

**Ethiopia:** Shari'a and customary courts are integrated into the judicial structure with defined jurisdiction. NJROS does not integrate jirgas into the judiciary but adopts Ethiopia's lesson that **defined jurisdiction prevents overreach**.

**Indonesia:** *Adat* (customary) institutions are recognized but subordinate to national law. Mapping exercises in Maluku and Papua demonstrate that registration improves data without eliminating custom.

**United Kingdom:** Community mediation schemes operate under **Civil Mediation Council** standards. While not jirga-equivalent, the accreditation model informs NJROS member registration and performance scoring.

**Conclusion:** No country has implemented a fully digital, criminal-detection-integrated jirga registry. NJROS would position Pakistan as a pioneer in plural-legal-system modernization—provided implementation respects community legitimacy.

## 2.8 FATA Merger and Institutional Continuity

The Twenty-Fifth Amendment (2018) merged FATA with Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, extending the Constitution and provincial courts to former tribal districts. Transition remains incomplete:

- **Parallel authority:** Jirgas and *jirga-like* bodies continue to adjudicate matters formerly handled under the Frontier Crimes Regulation (FCR), now repealed;
- **Police integration:** Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Police expansion into merged districts is ongoing; NJROS provides immediate case intelligence during institutional handover;
- **Land and inheritance:** FCR-era communal land arrangements create disputes ill-suited to sudden court adjudication; registered jirgas can facilitate lawful transition with recorded decisions enforceable in civil courts;
- **Women's access:** NGOs report persistent exclusion of women from proceedings in merged districts; NJROS victim reporting channels address this without requiring women's physical presence at male-dominated jirgas.

The Ministry of States and Frontier Regions (SAFRON) and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa government should prioritize NJROS pilot deployment in merged districts as a governance consolidation tool.

## 2.10 Evidence Base and Data Limitations

Rigorous national statistics on jirga activity do not exist—precisely because proceedings are unrecorded. This proposal relies on:

- Supreme Court and High Court case law (2010–2025);
- Human Rights Commission of Pakistan (HRCP) annual reports;
- Aurat Foundation and AGHS Legal Aid Cell case documentation;
- UN Women Pakistan programming evaluations;
- Provincial ombudsman complaints (KP, Balochistan);
- Media investigations (Dawn, The News, Tribal News Network).

NJROS itself will generate the first authoritative national dataset. Pilot phase baselines must be established through household surveys and NGO referral counts before impact claims are finalized.

# 3. CURRENT PROBLEMS

## 3.1 Overview

The unregulated operation of jirgas generates systemic governance failures across human rights, criminal justice, land administration, and gender equality. The problems documented below are drawn from judicial records, civil society reports, media investigations, and provincial ombudsman findings. They are presented thematically for policy clarity.

## 3.2 Concealment of Sexual Violence

In numerous cases across Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Balochistan, and Punjab, jirgas have brokered "settlements" following rape or gang rape. Typical patterns include:

1. The victim's family is pressured to withdraw complaints or refrain from filing an FIR;
2. The perpetrator's family pays compensation (*naqdi*, livestock, or land);
3. The victim is compelled to marry the perpetrator or a male relative of the perpetrator;
4. Community elders declare the matter "closed" (*sulah*).

Such outcomes violate the PPC (Sections 375–376), the Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act, and the State's non-derogable duty to prosecute rape regardless of private settlement. Without a public record, prosecutors lack evidence of coercion; victims remain isolated; and repeat offending continues.

**Illustrative harm:** A 2023 case in Mardan involved a jirga ordering a rape survivor to marry her assailant in exchange for PKR 800,000 paid to her father. The FIR was never registered until a whistleblower alerted media. No central record of the jirga's composition or decision existed.

## 3.3 Murder and Unlawful Compromise

Jirgas frequently adjudicate homicide under tribal custom, imposing **diyat** (blood money) or **qisas** arrangements without reference to Anti-Terrorism Court jurisdiction, vulnerable victim protections, or the Islamic provisions of the Qanun-e-Shahadat and PPC Chapter XVI.

Problems include:

- **Forced forgiveness:** Bereaved families compelled through *jirga* pressure or economic dependency to sign compromise deeds;
- **Underage beneficiaries:** Minors represented without lawful guardian or court approval;
- **Organized crime shielding:** Murder committed by influential tribal leaders "settled" without trial;
- **Feud escalation:** Incomplete settlements triggering cyclical retaliatory killings.

The State's interest in prosecuting murder is extinguished neither by custom nor by private agreement in cases attracting mandatory state prosecution or public-order implications.

## 3.4 Swara and Vani

**Swara** (also **Vani** in Punjab) refers to the practice of giving a girl, often a minor, in marriage to an aggrieved party or their family to resolve a dispute—frequently arising from a crime committed by a male member of the girl's family. Despite the Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act criminalizing Swara with imprisonment up to seven years, the practice persists.

Unregistered jirgas are the primary venue for Swara imposition. Decisions are oral, witnessed only by male elders, and unreported. Girls are transferred across districts without NADRA marriage registration scrutiny. School attendance records and health data often provide the only indirect evidence of the harm.

## 3.5 Forced Marriage and Ghag

**Ghag**—the declaration that a woman is betrothed without her consent—has been outlawed in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa but continues in jirga proceedings. Forced marriages to resolve debt, elopement, or "honour" allegations are routinely ratified by jirgas without reference to the **Muslim Family Laws Ordinance, 1961**, or the **Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Child Marriage Restraint Act, 2013**.

## 3.6 Denial of Inheritance

Women and marginalized heirs are disproportionately affected. Jirgas have:

- Declared daughters or widows "estranged" from the deceased to extinguish inheritance claims;
- Redistributed estate assets according to tribal custom contrary to Islamic succession law as codified in the **Muslim Family Laws Ordinance** and **West Pakistan Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1962**;
- Pressured heirs to execute relinquishment deeds under duress.

Without recorded proceedings, civil courts face evidentiary barriers when victims later seek redress.

## 3.7 Land Grabbing and Boundary Disputes

Jirgas adjudicate land disputes involving patwari records, oral tenancy, and collective tribal land (*shamilat*). Coercive outcomes include:

- Forced surrender of occupancy rights;
- Physical eviction enforced by jirga-appointed enforcers (*chowkidars* or tribal militias);
- Falsified boundary agreements later presented in civil courts as "community verdicts."

Land records digitization under Punjab and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa initiatives is undermined when parallel jirga "title decisions" circulate without registration.

## 3.8 Other Illegal Decisions

Additional documented harms include:

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Acid attack compromise** | Perpetrators released from accountability through payment |
| **Child custody** | Custody awarded without welfare assessment or court order |
| **Blasphemy accusations** | Community punishment ordered extrajudicially |
| **Labour and bonded labour** | Disputes resolved through continued servitude |
| **Timber and minerals** | Illegal extraction rights allocated |
| **Political intimidation** | Jirgas weaponized to suppress dissent |

## 3.9 Lack of Public Record

The absence of any mandatory record-keeping means:

- No statistical basis for policymaking;
- No chain of custody for decisions later litigated;
- No identification of repeat-offender jirga members;
- No early warning for law enforcement;
- No transparency for journalists, researchers, or UN mechanisms.

## 3.10 Lack of Accountability

Jirga members operate without professional standards, codes of conduct, or disciplinary mechanisms. Malpractice carries no license revocation because no license exists. Victims lack a clear complaints channel. Police often defer to elders' authority. District administration (*zaildar*, *assistant commissioner*) intervention is ad hoc.

## 3.11 Human Rights Violations: Systemic Analysis

The cumulative effect of unregulated jirgas constitutes a **systemic human rights risk** under international law:

- **Right to life** (Constitution Article 9; ICCPR Article 6);
- **Prohibition of torture and cruel treatment** (Article 14; CAT);
- **Right to liberty and security of person** (ICCPR Article 9);
- **Rights of the child** (CRC Articles 19, 24, 34, 37);
- **Non-discrimination and women's rights** (CEDAW Articles 2, 5, 16).

The UN Working Group on discrimination against women has specifically noted that informal justice mechanisms must comply with international human rights standards. NJROS operationalizes this requirement.

## 3.12 Impacts on Formal Justice and Rule of Law

Unregistered jirgas:

- Delay or deny access to courts (Article 10A);
- Corrupt the integrity of FIR registration;
- Burden superior courts with suo motu and constitutional petitions;
- Erode public confidence in state institutions;
- Create unequal justice: urban citizens use courts; rural citizens are subject to unwritten jirga law.

## 3.13 Illustrative Case Compendium

The following composite cases—drawn from documented patterns in judicial and civil society records—illustrate systemic failure. Names and locations are representative; specifics vary across incidents.

### Case 1: Rape Settlement and Forced Marriage (Khyber Pakhtunkhwa)

A 17-year-old survivor reported sexual assault by a man from a politically connected family. Before an FIR could be registered, a jirga convened at the *hujra* of a local elder. The survivor's father, a tenant farmer, was threatened with eviction. The jirga ordered: (a) PKR 1.2 million "compensation" to the survivor's family; (b) marriage of the survivor to the accused within 30 days; (c) withdrawal of any court petition. The marriage was not registered with NADRA. The survivor's school attendance ceased. A teacher's anonymous letter to the DPO eventually triggered belated FIR registration—six months after the jirga. The accused was acquitted in one trial citing "lack of evidence" and marriage complications. **NJROS intervention point:** Mandatory Category K-03 flag; P1 police notification; decision recording blocked; Women's Crisis Center alert.

### Case 2: Homicide Compromise and Feud Escalation (Balochistan)

Two sub-clans disputed water access. A confrontation left one man dead. A Tier-3 jirga imposed diyat of 30 goats and PKR 2 million, declaring *sulh*. The deceased's widow was not consulted. Six months later, the deceased's brother killed a member of the compensating clan, citing inadequate honour restoration. A second jirga imposed reciprocal diyat. Police filed no FIRs in either killing. Four additional deaths followed over two years. **NJROS intervention point:** K-02 automatic lock; prosecutor consultation; widow mandatory party status; feud tag triggering district administration early warning.

### Case 3: Swara Imposed for Uncle's Offence (Punjab–KPK border)

A man eloped with a married woman. His niece, aged 14, was given in *Vani/Swara* to the aggrieved husband's family to "restore honour." The girl's father signed a handwritten agreement witnessed only by male elders. Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act charges were filed only after HRCP intervention. The jirga members were not identified—"the community decided." **NJROS intervention point:** F-07 Critical flag; child protection unit; member JMID traceability; criminal referral for all signatories.

### Case 4: Inheritance Denial (Sindh)

Upon a landlord's death, a jirga of male cousins declared his daughters "disowned" due to their marriages to men from another tribe. Agricultural land was reallocated to sons. Daughters' counsel later obtained a civil court decree—but only after 11 years of litigation enabled by the jirga's initial "finality." **NJROS intervention point:** C-05 with woman complainant tag; inheritance rights information sheet auto-issued; 60-day cooling period before decision recording for inheritance cases.

### Case 5: Land Grabbing with Armed Enforcement (Khyber Pakhtunkhwa)

A jirga ruled that a widow must surrender 12 kanals to a tribal leader who claimed ancestral possession without patwari record. Within one week, armed men demolished her boundary wall. Police cited "civil dispute resolved by jirga." **NJROS intervention point:** C-01 with armed actors tag; land record API cross-check; DMO inspection order; criminal K-05 if theft of possession established.

## 3.14 Structural Enablers of Harm

Harmful jirga outcomes are not random. Structural enablers include:

1. **Patriarchal composition:** Overwhelmingly male panels deciding women's rights;
2. **Economic coercion:** Tenancy, debt, and employment dependence on elders;
3. **Political patronage:** Electoral candidates endorsing jirga authority for votes;
4. **Police deference:** Station House Officers treating elder calls as superior to complainant rights;
5. **Information asymmetry:** Parties unaware of statutory rights and FIR entitlements;
6. **Speed advantage:** Jirgas resolve in days; courts take years—creating perverse incentive to use jirgas even for criminal matters;
7. **Social ostracism:** *Budnaam* (dishonour) threats against families who reject jirga jurisdiction.

NJROS addresses each enabler: member registration (1), documented proceedings for court challenge (4–5), police notification (4), rights notices (5), digital efficiency for lawful civil cases (6), whistleblower protection (7).

## 3.16 Gender Analysis

Women and girls disproportionately bear costs of unregulated jirgas:

- Used as **commodities of exchange** in Swara/Vani;
- **Excluded from proceedings** affecting their marriage, custody, and inheritance;
- **Silenced** through *purdah* norms and retaliation fears;
- **Re-victimized** by marriage to perpetrators;
- **Denied inheritance** through male-only deliberation.

NJROS gender-responsive design includes: victim-centric reporting; critical flags for woman complainant; women's member incentive; mandatory crisis center alerts; gender-disaggregated public dashboard data.

# 5. SCOPE

## 5.1 Geographic Scope

NJROS shall apply to the **entire territory of Pakistan**, including:

- All provinces: Punjab, Sindh, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Balochistan;
- Islamabad Capital Territory;
- Gilgit-Baltistan;
- Azad Jammu and Kashmir (subject to constitutional arrangement and local legislation).

## 5.2 Institutional Scope

NJROS covers:

| Included | Excluded (with rationale) |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Traditional jirgas (Pashtun, Baloch, etc.) | Formal courts of law |
| Community panchayats exercising binding authority | Court-annexed mediation under ADR Acts |
| Tribal councils issuing dispute decisions | Arbitration under the Arbitration Act, 1940 (unless overlapping—see 5.4) |
| Faith-community councils acting as jirgas | Fatwa councils without binding dispute powers |
| Urban neighborhood dispute committees mimicking jirga structure | Licensed law firms and advocates |

## 5.3 Subject-Matter Scope

NJROS governs proceedings addressing:

- Civil disputes (land, debt, contract, inheritance);
- Family disputes (marriage, divorce, custody, maintenance);
- Criminal or quasi-criminal matters (assault, homicide, sexual offences, theft);
- Community and inter-clan conflicts;
- Resource disputes (water, forestry, grazing).

## 5.4 Overlap with ADR and Arbitration

Where a proceeding is styled as "arbitration" or "ADR" but functions in substance as a jirga—convened by elders, applying custom, enforcing social compliance—NJROS registration shall be **mandatory**. The Monitoring Authority may issue guidance distinguishing lawful ADR from disguised jirga activity.

## 5.5 Personal Scope

All **jirga conveners**, **members**, **secretaries/recorders**, and **parties** to proceedings fall within scope. Third-party enforcers (*chowkidars*, militias) identified in decisions shall be flagged to law enforcement.

## 5.6 Temporal Scope

Upon enactment of the enabling legislation:

- **Phase-in period (90 days):** Registration open; proceedings may continue with grace-period warnings;
- **From Day 91:** Convening an unregistered jirga or failing to file a case record constitutes an offence;
- **Retrospective:** No criminal liability for past unregistered proceedings; civil claims remain unaffected.

## 5.7 Functional Boundaries with Formal Courts

NJROS jirgas shall **not** exercise the following (reserved to courts):

- Issuance of legally binding divorce decrees (*khula* judicial approval);
- Grant of probate and letters of administration;
- Trial of Schedule A offences;
- Contempt enforcement against non-parties;
- Issuance of injunctions enforceable through court contempt power.

Registered jirga decisions in permitted categories may be **filed as mediated settlements** for court endorsement under proposed Section 16 of the NJROS Act, creating enforceable titles without parallel sovereignty.

## 5.8 Urban and Diaspora Dimensions

Urban katchi abadi dispute committees and overseas Pakistani community jirgas (e.g., UK/UAE-based panels directing domestic family decisions) fall within scope where:

- Decisions affect persons or property in Pakistan; or
- Parties are Pakistani nationals and the matter would constitute a jirga if convened domestically.

Enforcement against diaspora conveners operates through travel restrictions or legal notice via MOFA where legally permissible—policy detail subject to feasibility study.

## 5.9 Exemptions (Limited)

The following are **exempt** unless they issue binding dispute decisions mimicking jirgas:

- Informal neighborly mediation without enforcement claims;
- Internal trade union or professional association disciplinary meetings;
- School and university disciplinary committees;
- Family discussions without elder-imposed binding verdict.

NJMA may issue clarificatory notifications on borderline cases.

# 6. GUIDING PRINCIPLES

## 6.1 Constitutional Supremacy

No jirga decision shall contravene the Constitution. Fundamental rights are non-negotiable floor protections.

## 6.2 Legality and Non-Derogation

Custom (*riwaj*) cannot override statute in matters where Parliament or provincial assemblies have legislated. Criminal law is uniformly applicable.

## 6.3 Human Rights Centrality

Every NJROS process shall prioritize the safety, dignity, and legal agency of victims—especially women, children, minorities, and persons with disabilities.

## 6.4 Cultural Respect and Community Ownership

Regulation shall be implemented through consultation with elders, women's organizations, bar councils, and religious scholars. Terminology and portal design shall reflect local languages (Urdu, Pashto, Balochi, Punjabi, Sindhi, Brahui).

## 6.5 Transparency by Default

Proceedings and decisions are recorded. Redaction protects victim identity in sensitive cases, but the State retains full access.

## 6.6 Accountability and Proportionality

Sanctions for non-compliance shall be proportionate: warnings, suspension, de-registration, fines, and criminal referral for egregious cases.

## 6.7 Technology Neutrality and Inclusion

Digital-first does not mean digital-only. NJROS field offices and mobile units shall serve areas with low connectivity. Offline capture with synchronized upload is mandatory.

## 6.8 Data Protection and Privacy

Personal data shall be processed per the **Personal Data Protection Bill** (upon enactment) and NJROS regulations. Biometric data of members links to NADRA with consent.

## 6.9 Federal-Provincial Coordination

Criminal law and police are provincial subjects post-18th Amendment; NJROS requires cooperative federalism via Council of Common Interests (CCI) or Inter-Provincial Coordination Committee (IPCC) protocols.

## 6.10 Victim-Centered Design

Victims may report jirgas covertly through SMS, hotline, or women's crisis centers. Whistleblower protections apply.

## 6.11 Presumption of Good Faith

Registered jirgas operating compliantly receive recognition benefits (see Chapter 19). The system punishes malpractice, not tradition.

## 6.12 Continuous Improvement

Machine-learning models, scoring algorithms, and risk matrices shall be audited annually for bias against regions, tribes, or genders.

## 6.13 Subsidiarity and Local Autonomy

Tier 1 jirgas retain maximum autonomy in lawful civil dispute resolution within registered jurisdiction. Federal and provincial oversight intensifies only with risk score elevation—respecting the principle that local disputes are best resolved locally when rights-compliant.

## 6.14 Do No Harm

Implementation shall avoid sudden disruption of functioning civil sulh during pilot phase. Grace periods, elder ambassadors, and parallel operation of provisional registration prevent governance shock in fragile districts.

## 6.15 Intersectionality

NJROS tags and protection protocols shall account for intersecting vulnerabilities: woman + minority religion + disability + child status trigger compounded review, not single-factor analysis.

# 7. REGISTRATION PROCESS

## 7.1 Overview

Registration is the gateway to lawful jirga operation. Unregistered jirgas may not issue decisions with any legal or social recognition under NJROS rules, and members face liability for convening knowingly.

## 7.2 Registration Tiers

| Tier | Description | Approval Authority |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Tier 1 – Community Jirga** | Village or neighborhood level; ≤7 members | District Monitoring Officer |
| **Tier 2 – Tehsil Jirga** | Multi-village; complex civil disputes | Deputy Commissioner |
| **Tier 3 – Council Jirga** | District-level; inter-tribal matters | Commissioner + Monitoring Authority regional director |

## 7.3 Application Requirements

A jirga applying for registration shall submit:

1. **Application form** (NJROS-REG-01) via portal or field office;
2. **List of proposed members** (minimum 3, maximum 15) with CNIC, NADRA verification, criminal record self-declaration;
3. **Geographic jurisdiction** (GPS polygon or revenue estate list);
4. **Constitution/charter** adopting the NJROS Code of Conduct;
5. **Designated secretary** with basic literacy and smartphone capability (training provided);
6. **Community endorsement** from at least two of: local government chair, union council, women's group, bar association representative;
7. **Oath of compliance** signed by each member (digital or witnessed physical).

## 7.4 Vetting Process

1. **Automated checks:** NADRA identity; NACTA watchlist; pending FIRs (via PSRMS integration);
2. **Manual review:** District Monitoring Officer examines endorsements and jurisdiction overlaps;
3. **Site verification (Tier 2/3):** Field visit confirming community legitimacy;
4. **Decision within 21 working days**; deemed approval if no response (with post-audit);
5. **Registration certificate** issued with unique **Jirga Registration Number (JRN)** and QR code.

## 7.5 Renewal and Amendment

- Annual renewal mandatory;
- Member changes reported within 7 days;
- Jurisdiction expansion requires re-approval;
- Failure to renew triggers automatic suspension.

## 7.6 De-Registration

Voluntary or compulsory de-registration upon dissolution, repeated violations, or community petition with substantiated evidence.

## 7.7 Provisional Registration (Pilot Phase)

During the first 12 months in pilot districts, **provisional registration** may be granted for 90 days pending full vetting—to maximize uptake while building records.

## 7.8 Detailed Registration Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)

### SOP-REG-001: Application Intake

**Step 1 — Portal Access:** Applicant (convener or designated secretary) creates NJROS account using CNIC and mobile OTP verification linked to NADRA.

**Step 2 — Form Completion:** All mandatory fields validated in real time. Incomplete applications saved as draft for 30 days.

**Step 3 — Document Upload:** Scanned community endorsements (PDF/JPEG, max 5MB each); jurisdiction map via GeoJSON polygon tool or revenue estate multiselect from land record integration where available.

**Step 4 — Fee Payment:** Registration fee waived in pilot phase. Post-pilot: Tier 1 PKR 2,000; Tier 2 PKR 5,000; Tier 3 PKR 10,000 (revenue to NJMA provincial operations).

**Step 5 — Submission Confirmation:** SMS and email with tracking ID (*REG-YYYY-NNNNNN*).

### SOP-REG-002: Vetting and Approval

**Day 1–3:** Automated NADRA, NACTA, and PSRMS checks. Flags routed to DMO queue.

**Day 4–14:** DMO reviews endorsements, interviews convener by phone or field visit (Tier 2/3 mandatory field visit).

**Day 15–21:** Approval, conditional approval (deficiency cure within 14 days), or reasoned rejection. Applicant may appeal to Regional Director within 15 days.

**Upon Approval:** JRN issued; certificate PDF with QR; member JMIDs activated; training enrollment link sent.

### SOP-REG-003: Case Opening Pre-Condition

No case may be opened on NJROS until jirga holds **active** registration status. System hard-blocks case creation for suspended or expired JRNs.

### SOP-REG-004: Public Verification

Any citizen may scan JRN QR code to verify registration status, tier, jurisdiction, and suspension status—**not** member personal addresses (privacy).

## 7.9 Registration Appeals

Rejected applicants may appeal within 15 days to the NJMA Regional Director. Decision within 30 days. Further appeal to NJMA Chairperson on questions of law or procedural fairness.

## 7.10 Inter-Jurisdictional Disputes

Where two registered jirgas claim overlapping jurisdiction, DMO mediates within 21 days. Pending mediation, neither jirga may record decisions in disputed territory. NJMA may redraw boundaries with community consultation.

# 10. CRIMINAL CASE DETECTION

## 10.1 Purpose

Automated and human-in-the-loop detection prevents jirgas from serving as forums to launder criminal liability.

## 10.2 Detection Layers

### Layer 1: Mandatory Classification

Secretaries must select case type. Misclassification is an offence if proven deliberate.

### Layer 2: Rules Engine

Keyword and field-based rules flag cases when narratives contain terms (configurable per language): *qatl, zina, izzat, swara, vani, ghag, baligh nahi, jabar*, etc.

### Layer 3: NLP/ML Assist (Phase 2)

Urdu and Pashto natural language processing models trained on anonymized case text suggest criminal relevance scores. **Human review mandatory** before police notification for ML-only hits to reduce false positives.

### Layer 4: Third-Party Reports

Victims, NGOs, or anonymous tips can flag a case ID or unregistered gathering.

### Layer 5: Cross-System Checks

If party CNIC matches open FIR, PSRMS warrant, or court case, automatic Critical flag.

## 10.3 Risk Scoring Matrix

| Score | Action |
|-------|--------|
| 0–30 (Low) | Standard monitoring |
| 31–60 (Medium) | District Monitoring Officer weekly review |
| 61–85 (High) | Hold on decision recording; mandatory consultation with prosecutor |
| 86–100 (Critical) | Immediate police notification; jirga may not record settlement |

## 10.4 Schedule A Offences

A statutory Schedule A listing PPC sections non-compoundable and non-delegable to jirga shall be maintained by the Monitoring Authority with annual parliamentary review.

## 10.5 Audit and Override

District Monitoring Officers may upgrade or downgrade scores with written justification. Overrides audited monthly at regional level.

## 10.6 Schedule A Offences — Indicative List

The following Pakistan Penal Code provisions shall appear in Schedule A (non-exhaustive; subject to parliamentary approval):

| PPC Section | Offence | NJROS Treatment |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| 302 | Murder | Auto-lock; P1 notification |
| 304 | Culpable homicide | Auto-lock; prosecutor review |
| 375–376 | Rape | Auto-lock; P1; victim protection |
| 354 | Assault on modesty | Auto-lock |
| 363–369 | Kidnapping/abduction | Auto-lock |
| 337-A to 337-F | Hurt (grievous) | Auto-lock or hold |
| 489-A | Acid throwing | Auto-lock; P1 |
| Prevention of Anti-Women Practices Act offences | Swara, Vani, etc. | Auto-lock; criminal referral |

Compoundable offences under **CrPC Section 345** may proceed to recorded *sulh* only after sessions court or magistrate approval entered in NJROS—system blocks until court order number uploaded.

## 10.7 Natural Language Processing (Phase 2) — Safeguards

Urdu and Pashto NLP models shall:

- Operate on **anonymized** training corpora;
- Undergo bias testing for tribal name, dialect, and gender false positives;
- Never auto-notify police without human DMO confirmation for ML-only triggers;
- Be explainable: keyword hits displayed to reviewing officer;
- Be audited quarterly by independent IT ethics panel including linguists from KP and Balochistan universities.

## 10.8 Whistleblower and Covert Reporting Channel

Citizens may report unregistered jirgas or concealed criminal matters via:

- **Toll-free hotline** 1099-NJROS (proposed);
- **SMS:** `REPORT [district code] [brief text]` to 8399;
- **Pakistan Citizen Portal** integration;
- **In-person** at DMO office, police station (protected disclosure), or women's crisis center.

Reports generate **Covert Case ID (CC-ID)** not visible to named jirga until DMO validates. Retaliation against whistleblowers punishable under proposed NJROS Act Section 42 (indicative): up to 2 years imprisonment.

## 10.9 Detection Performance Targets

| Metric | Pilot target | National target (Year 3) |
|--------|--------------|---------------------------|
| True positive rate (Schedule A cases flagged) | ≥85% | ≥92% |
| False positive rate (unnecessary police alerts) | ≤20% | ≤10% |
| Time from flag to police ack (P1) | ≤2 hours | ≤1 hour |
| Unregistered jirga reports investigated | ≥70% within 14 days | ≥90% within 7 days |

# 11. POLICE NOTIFICATION PROCESS

## 11.1 Legal Basis

Upon enactment, NJROS police notification satisfies the **information to police** requirement under CrPC Section 154 read with Section 157 for cognizable cases discovered through official channels.

## 11.2 Notification Workflow

```
Case Flagged (Score ≥61)
        ↓
Automated alert to District Police Officer (DPO) dashboard + SMS
        ↓
Designated NJROS Police Liaison Officer acknowledges within 30 minutes
        ↓
Liaison reviews case packet (parties, narrative, location, jirga JRN)
        ↓
If cognizable: FIR registration initiated; NJROS case locked
        ↓
FIR number entered in NJROS within 24 hours
        ↓
Prosecution and Monitoring Authority receive copy
```

## 11.3 Priority Levels

| Priority | Trigger | Response Time |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| P1 | Sexual offence, child, homicide | 2 hours |
| P2 | Assault, kidnapping, acid | 6 hours |
| P3 | Other cognizable | 24 hours |

## 11.4 Non-Response Protocol

Failure to acknowledge P1 within 2 hours escalates to Regional Police Officer and Monitoring Authority. Repeated failures trigger report to Inspector General and provincial ombudsman.

## 11.5 Victim Protection

Simultaneous alert to **Women's Crisis Center**, **Child Protection Unit**, or **Human Rights Ministry helpline** when case type warrants.

## 11.6 Jirga Member Obligations

Members discovering criminal conduct mid-proceeding must cease adjudication and file NJROS incident report within 6 hours. Failure is grounds for suspension and criminal accessory liability where applicable.

## 11.7 Integration with PSRMS

Bi-directional API: FIR status updates flow back to NJROS; prevents "closed" jirga decisions when prosecution active.

## 11.8 Detailed Police Notification SOP (SOP-POL-001)

### 11.8.1 Alert Payload Contents

Every police notification shall include:

1. NJROS Case ID and JRN;
2. Risk score and triggering rules;
3. Party CNICs (hashed in SMS; full in secure dashboard);
4. Case narrative (Urdu and English);
5. GPS coordinates of jirga meeting if available;
6. Member JMIDs;
7. Recommended PPC sections (system-suggested; officer verifies);
8. Vulnerability tags (minor, woman complainant, etc.);
9. Timestamp of flag and lock status.

### 11.8.2 Roles and Responsibilities

| Actor | Responsibility |
|-------|----------------|
| NJROS System | Auto-generate alert; lock decision recording |
| NJROS Police Liaison Officer (NPLO) | First acknowledgement; quality review |
| Station House Officer | FIR registration if cognizable |
| DPO | Escalation oversight; P1 non-response |
| Prosecutor | Consultation on compoundable matters |
| DMO | Parallel administrative review |
| NJMA | Monthly compliance audit on police response times |

### 11.8.3 FIR Registration Protocol

Upon cognizable determination:

1. NPLO or SHO registers FIR within statutory time limits;
2. FIR number entered in NJROS within 24 hours;
3. Case status changes to **CRIMINAL_REFERRED**;
4. Parties notified via SMS that jirga proceedings are suspended for criminal matters;
5. Jirga members receive automated notice: continuing proceedings constitutes offence.

### 11.8.4 Non-Cognizable and Compoundable Pathways

Where offence is non-cognizable or compoundable with court permission:

- Prosecutor uploads **No Further Action** or **Court Compromise Order** to unlock NJROS for limited civil residual matters not touching criminal liability;
- System maintains audit link between court order and any subsequent sulh recording.

### 11.8.5 Provincial Variations

KP, Punjab, Sindh, and Balochistan Police shall designate **one NPLO per district** minimum. Islamabad and GB/AJK establish equivalent liaison under local police structure. NPLO training: 40-hour NJROS-Police joint curriculum at Police Training Colleges.

### 11.8.6 Performance Monitoring

Inspector General offices receive monthly dashboards:

- Alerts received vs. FIRs registered;
- Mean response time by district;
- NPLO non-acknowledgement count;
- Correlation with subsequent crime reports in same JRN jurisdiction.

Districts in bottom quartile for compliance subject to NJMA report to provincial Public Safety Commission.

# 12. DECISION RECORDING

## 12.1 Requirement

Every registered jirga proceeding that advances beyond preliminary meeting must have a **Decision Record (DR)** or **Proceedings Summary (PS)** filed within 48 hours of adjournment.

## 12.2 Decision Record Components

1. Case ID and hearing dates;
2. Attendance log (CNIC-verified parties and members);
3. Summary of claims and responses;
4. Evidence references (documents, witnesses—no secret evidence);
5. Decision text in plain language;
6. Compliance timeline;
7. Member digital signatures (minimum quorum: 60% of registered members);
8. Party acknowledgment or recorded refusal;
9. Appeal rights notice (auto-generated).

## 12.3 Enforceability

NJROS-registered decisions in **Category C and non-critical F** may be filed in civil courts as mediated settlements under proposed enforcement provisions. **Category K decisions are void ab initio** if recorded in violation of lock rules.

## 12.4 Immutability

Once submitted, DRs cannot be edited. Corrections require supplementary filing with audit trail.

## 12.5 Oral-Only Proceedings Prohibited

Decisions issued without NJROS recording are **non-compliant** regardless of community enforcement.

## 12.6 Language

Decisions recorded in language of proceedings; Urdu summary mandatory.

## 12.7 Decision Recording Standard Operating Procedure (SOP-DR-001)

### 12.7.1 Pre-Decision Checks

Before any decision is entered, the system automatically verifies:

1. JRN active and not suspended;
2. Case not locked for criminal flag;
3. Quorum of registered members present (≥60%);
4. All parties CNIC-verified or exception approved by DMO;
5. Mandatory cooling periods elapsed (inheritance: 60 days; F-06 Ghag: 14 days minimum);
6. No open P1 police alert unacknowledged.

Failure of any check blocks submission and displays remedial instructions to secretary.

### 12.7.2 Hearing Documentation

Each hearing session requires a **Proceedings Summary (PS)** filed within 24 hours:

- Date, time, location (GPS optional);
- Members present (JMID checklist);
- Parties or representatives present;
- Summary of oral arguments (minimum 100 words);
- Adjournment reason if applicable.

Multiple PS records chain into single Decision Record upon final adjudication.

### 12.7.3 Decision Content Standards

Decision text must:

- State findings of fact in neutral language;
- Cite applicable custom, statute, or Sharia principles invoked (without contradicting statute);
- Specify obligations of each party with deadlines;
- Include compliance monitoring clause (default: 30 days);
- Attach list of witnesses if credibility findings depend on testimony;
- **Not** contain orders for Swara, Vani, forced marriage, or withdrawal of criminal complaints.

Automated NLP screen rejects prohibited terms; manual DMO review for circumlocutions (e.g., "marriage as compensation").

### 12.7.4 Party Acknowledgment

Parties digitally acknowledge or record refusal:

- **Acknowledged:** Compliance clock starts;
- **Refused:** Decision still filed but flagged for potential appeal; jirga may not use social enforcement pending appeal window (30 days)—violation reportable.

Where party is illiterate, voice recording with consent uploaded; independent witness JMID required.

### 12.7.5 Enforcement Pathways

| Decision type | Enforcement mechanism |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Civil payment | Civil court filing upon non-compliance |
| Land boundary | Patwari intimation + civil court |
| Sulh (lawful) | Social recognition + court endorsement option |
| Family maintenance | Family court filing |
| Criminal (void) | Not enforceable; police referral |

### 12.7.6 Archival and Evidentiary Weight

Decision Records carry cryptographic hash stored in audit ledger. Courts may take judicial notice of NJROS records as **prima facie** evidence of what was decided and by whom—reversing current evidentiary vacuum.

# 13. MONITORING AUTHORITY

## 13.1 Establishment

The **National Jirga Monitoring Authority (NJMA)** shall be established as an autonomous federal body under the administrative control of the Ministry of Law and Justice.

## 13.2 Governance Structure

| Position | Appointment |
|----------|-------------|
| Chairperson | Retired judge of High Court or Supreme Court |
| Members (5) | Law, human rights, IT, police, Islamic jurisprudence |
| Provincial Directors (4) | Seconded senior civil servants |
| Victim Advocate Panel | Rotating civil society representatives |

## 13.3 Powers and Functions

- Register, renew, suspend, and de-register jirgas;
- Audit case files and conduct surprise inspections;
- Issue binding compliance orders;
- Refer matters to FIA, police, and prosecutors;
- Impose administrative fines up to PKR 500,000;
- Publish annual State of Customary Justice Report;
- Maintain Schedule A and detection rules;
- Operate whistleblower hotline.

## 13.4 District Monitoring Officers (DMOs)

One DMO per district (or shared across low-volume districts) responsible for first-line oversight, registration approval, and community liaison.

## 13.5 Reporting Lines

NJMA reports to Parliament annually via Ministry of Law. Provincial assemblies receive provincial annexes.

## 13.6 Independence Safeguards

Fixed tenure, removal only for cause, separate budget line, no instruction on individual case outcomes from executive.

# 15. PERFORMANCE SCORE SYSTEM

## 15.1 Purpose

Incentivize compliant, rights-respecting dispute resolution through transparent metrics.

## 15.2 Score Components (100 points)

| Component | Weight | Metrics |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Compliance | 30 | Timely filing, accurate classification, renewal |
| Quality | 25 | Appeal overturn rate, party satisfaction surveys |
| Human rights | 25 | Zero confirmed violations; training completion |
| Efficiency | 10 | Median resolution time vs. benchmark |
| Community | 10 | Endorsements, women's participation |

## 15.3 Score Bands

| Band | Score | Benefits |
|------|-------|----------|
| Green | 80–100 | Fast-track registration; public recognition; court filing fee waiver for enforced settlements |
| Amber | 60–79 | Standard operations; improvement plan |
| Red | 40–59 | Enhanced monitoring; mandatory retraining |
| Black | <40 | Suspension review triggered |

## 15.4 Appeals of Scores

Jirgas may appeal scores within 30 days. Independent review panel decides within 45 days.

## 15.5 Anti-Gaming

Artificially low resolution times or collusive party satisfaction trigger fraud investigation.

## 15.6 Detailed Scoring Methodology

### 15.6.1 Compliance Sub-Score (30 points)

| Metric | Points | Deduction trigger |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| Timely case filing (within 24h of meeting) | 10 | Late filing: -2 per incident |
| Timely decision recording (within 48h) | 10 | Late: -3 per incident |
| Annual renewal on time | 5 | Late renewal: -5 |
| Accurate case classification | 5 | Deliberate misclass: -10 + referral |

### 15.6.2 Quality Sub-Score (25 points)

| Metric | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Appeal overturn rate <5% | 15 |
| Party satisfaction survey ≥4/5 (random sample) | 10 |

Appeals upheld on human rights grounds deduct 8 points per incident.

### 15.6.3 Human Rights Sub-Score (25 points)

Starts at 25. Deductions:

- Confirmed rights violation: -25 (immediate Black band review);
- Critical flag mishandling: -15;
- Incomplete training: -5 per member;
- Women's participation incentive: +5 bonus (max 25).

### 15.6.4 Efficiency Sub-Score (10 points)

Median resolution time compared to district benchmark for case type. Top quartile: 10 points; bottom quartile: 2 points.

### 15.6.5 Community Sub-Score (10 points)

Based on: community petition support (no active petitions against: 5 points); NGO endorsement (5 points); documented collaboration with Legal Aid (2 bonus).

### 15.6.6 Publication of Scores

Jirga scores published quarterly on public dashboard (JRN level, not individual member names unless suspended) to foster reputational competition for Green band status.

# 16. SUSPENSION RULES

## 16.1 Grounds for Suspension

1. Operating while unregistered;
2. Recording decisions in locked Category K cases;
3. Confirmed Swara, Vani, or forced marriage facilitation;
4. Threatening parties to prevent NJROS filing;
5. Two or more High-risk score periods in 12 months;
6. Obstruction of Monitoring Authority audit;
7. Member disqualification affecting quorum.

## 16.2 Suspension Types

| Type | Duration | Effect |
|------|----------|--------|
| Interim | Up to 30 days | Pending investigation |
| Partial | 3–12 months | Specific case types barred |
| Full | 6 months–permanent | No proceedings |

## 16.3 Procedure

Show-cause notice → hearing within 14 days → written order → appeal to NJMA Appellate Bench.

## 16.4 Criminal Referral

Suspension does not preclude parallel criminal prosecution of members.

## 16.5 Community Remediation

Reinstatement after full suspension may require community assembly acknowledging harm and corrective charter—**victim consent required** where victim identifiable.

# 17. APPEALS PROCESS

## 17.1 Party Appeals

Parties dissatisfied with jirga decisions may appeal within **30 days** to:

1. **NJMA District Appellate Officer** (administrative review for procedural violations, human rights breaches, misclassification);
2. **Civil courts** (for civil matters) under proposed enforcement section;
3. **High Court** (constitutional petition where fundamental rights violated).

## 17.2 Grounds for Appeal

- Procedural irregularity (quorum, notice);
- Coercion or duress;
- Decision contrary to statute;
- Criminal matter improperly adjudicated;
- Bias or conflict of interest of members.

## 17.3 Appellate Powers

NJMA Appellate Officer may:

- Set aside decision;
- Remand for re-hearing with observer;
- Refer to police;
- Recommend member suspension.

## 17.4 Stays

Filing appeal does not automatically stay compliance unless Appellate Officer orders stay for risk of irreparable harm.

## 17.5 Legal Aid

Eligible appellants referred to Legal Aid and Justice Authority.

## 17.6 Time Standards

Appellate decision within **60 days**; extension with reasons communicated to parties.

## 17.7 Appellate Procedure Detail

### 17.7.1 Filing

Appellant submits NJROS-APL-01 with: Case ID; grounds; relief sought; evidence of coercion if applicable. Filing fee: waived for women, children, and Legal Aid certificate holders.

### 17.7.2 Interim Protection

Appellate Officer may order:

- Stay of decision enforcement;
- Temporary exclusion of respondent from appellant's residence (coordinated through police, not jirga);
- Referral to victim protection (Annex G).

### 17.7.3 Respondent Notice

Respondent and all jirga members receive notice within 3 working days. Members may file written response.

### 17.7.4 Hearing

Oral hearing within 21 days where liberty, child custody, or housing at stake. Video link permitted for appellants unable to travel safely.

### 17.7.5 Orders

Appellate Officer may: affirm; set aside; remand with NJMA observer; refer to police; recommend member suspension.

### 17.7.6 Further Recourse

NJMA Appellate Bench (3 members) hears appeals from Appellate Officer on law and jurisdiction within 45 days. Constitutional petitions remain available to High Courts regardless.

### 17.7.7 Statistical Targets

≤10% of decisions appealed nationally (indicates neither chaos nor captured silence); ≥30% of appeals in pilot districts brought by women or on behalf of minors (indicates access).

## 18.9 Islamic Jurisprudential Note

# 24. CONCLUSION

Pakistan stands at a defining juncture in its relationship between customary governance and constitutional democracy. The jirga is not merely a relic of the past; it remains a living institution that millions of citizens regard as legitimate, accessible, and culturally resonant. Yet the unchecked operation of unregistered jirgas has produced a shadow justice system in which the most vulnerable—women, girls, minorities, and the poor—bear the cost of opacity, and in which grave crimes are concealed beneath the language of reconciliation.

The **National Jirga Registration and Oversight System (NJROS)** offers a principled third path. It neither abolishes community wisdom nor surrenders the State's duty to protect life, dignity, and rights. Through mandatory registration, digital transparency, criminal case detection, police notification, and independent monitoring, NJROS aligns the jirga with the rule of law while preserving its restorative virtues.

The problems documented in this proposal—Swara and Vani, rape settlements, murder compromises, inheritance denial, land grabbing, and the absence of public record—are not hypothetical. They are documented failures of governance that demand a systemic response. Piecemeal interventions and sporadic judicial suo motu actions, however necessary, cannot substitute for a standing national architecture.

Implementation will require political courage, provincial cooperation, elder endorsement, and sustained investment. The risks of inaction, however, exceed the risks of reform. Every year without NJROS perpetuates impunity and deepens distrust in formal institutions.

The Government of Pakistan is therefore urged to:

1. **Approve NJROS in principle** and establish the National Jirga Monitoring Authority;
2. **Enact enabling legislation** with bipartisan and provincial support;
3. **Fund the five-year implementation roadmap** through PSDP and provincial partnership;
4. **Launch pilot districts** within twelve months of enactment;
5. **Report to Parliament annually** on progress toward a safer, more transparent customary justice system.

NJROS is not an attack on tradition. It is the condition under which tradition can survive with honor in a modern constitutional state—and under which no settlement, however ancient the ritual, may buy silence in the face of rape, blood, or the sale of a girl's future.

## 24.1 Call to Action

The Cabinet is respectfully requested to approve this proposal and authorize the Ministry of Law and Justice to:

1. Constitute an **Inter-Ministerial NJROS Task Force** within 14 days;
2. Allocate **seed funding of PKR 150 million** in the supplementary grant for FY 2026–27 for NJMA establishment and RFP preparation;
3. Refer draft legislation to the **Council of Common Interests** within 60 days;
4. Issue a **policy statement** clarifying that registration honors jirga legitimacy while protecting citizens;
5. Invite **provincial chief ministers** to nominate pilot districts within 30 days.

## 24.2 Parliamentary Consideration

Upon introduction, the National Assembly Standing Committee on Law and Justice should conduct public hearings in Islamabad, Peshawar, Quetta, Lahore, and Karachi—with dedicated women's-only sessions—to refine Schedule A and victim protection provisions without diluting mandatory registration.

## 24.3 Closing Statement

For too long, Pakistan has tolerated a bifurcated justice system: one written in statutes, another spoken in hujras and jirga tents. The victims of that bifurcation cannot wait for gradual social change alone. NJROS offers a disciplined, respectful, and modern path: **the jirga recorded, the criminal offence reported, the inheritance preserved, the girl protected, the murder not forgiven in secret.**

The choice before the Government is not between tradition and law. It is between **unregulated harm** and **regulated dignity**. NJROS chooses dignity.

# ANNEX F: CODE OF CONDUCT FOR REGISTERED JIRGA MEMBERS

Registered members shall adhere to the following binding code:

1. **Legality:** I shall not facilitate any decision contrary to the Constitution and laws of Pakistan.
2. **Human rights:** I shall not support Swara, Vani, forced marriage, or inheritance denial.
3. **Criminal matters:** I shall immediately cease proceedings and notify police when cognizable offences arise.
4. **Recording:** I shall ensure all proceedings are filed on NJROS within prescribed timelines.
5. **Impartiality:** I shall disclose conflicts of interest and recuse where appropriate.
6. **Dignity:** I shall treat all parties, especially women and children, with respect and ensure their voices are heard.
7. **Non-coercion:** I shall not threaten ostracism or violence to enforce compliance.
8. **Confidentiality:** I shall protect party privacy except where disclosure is legally required.
9. **Training:** I shall complete annual NJROS training.
10. **Cooperation:** I shall cooperate with NJMA inspections and provide accurate information.

Violation constitutes grounds for suspension, de-registration, and criminal referral where applicable.

# ANNEX G: VICTIM PROTECTION PROTOCOL (SUMMARY)

**Trigger:** Category F-07, F-08, K-03, K-07, K-09; or any case with minor party tag.

**Immediate actions (within 2 hours):**

1. NJROS auto-alert to nearest Women's Crisis Center / Child Protection Unit;
2. Case locked for settlement recording;
3. DMO assigned for welfare check within 24 hours if contact details available;
4. Police P1 notification parallel;
5. Legal Aid and Justice Authority referral SMS to victim or representative.

**Medium-term (7 days):**

- Risk assessment interview (female officer / NGO where victim is woman/girl);
- Safe shelter referral if retaliation risk HIGH;
- School continuation order communicated to district education officer for child victims.

**Documentation:** All protection actions logged in NJROS Victim Support Module—visible to NJMA, not to jirga members until investigation concludes.

# ANNEX L: FREQUENTLY ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS (GOVERNMENT BRIEFING)

**Q1: Does NJROS abolish jirgas?**  
No. Registration preserves lawful jirga operation with state recognition.

**Q2: Will elders lose authority?**  
Lawful authority is strengthened through Green band recognition. Unlawful authority over criminal matters is correctly removed.

**Q3: What if there is no internet?**  
Field offices, SMS, offline mobile sync, and paper with retroactive entry.

**Q4: Will police misuse alerts?**  
Escalation to IG, NPLO training, and parliamentary oversight apply.

**Q5: Can women participate?**  
Encouraged through incentives; victim reporting does not require jirga attendance.

**Q6: Is this constitutional?**  
Yes—under Articles 4, 9, 10A, 25, and provincial cooperative legislation.

**Q7: What about diyat and qisas?**  
Permitted only through court-supervised processes; jirgas may facilitate agreement but not replace courts for Schedule A matters.

**Q8: Cost to communities?**  
Tier 1 registration fee nominal (PKR 2,000 post-pilot); waived during pilot.
